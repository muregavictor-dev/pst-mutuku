Obedience

What Obedience Actually Costs

8 min read · Obedience

We talk about obedience as if it's a feeling — something you either have or don't on a given Sunday. It isn't. Obedience is a series of small, costly decisions made long before anyone is watching, and most of us quietly negotiate our way out of them.

The hardest part of obedience was never the believing. Most of the people I counsel believe exactly what scripture says. Their crisis isn't doctrinal — it's practical. They know what God has asked of them, and they are negotiating with the cost of actually doing it.

I think of obedience less as a single decision and more as a current — something you either swim with or against, every single day, in decisions too small to preach about. The marriage you stay faithful to when no one would know if you didn't. The business deal you walk away from because it required a small lie. The apology you give before you're ready to, because pride isn't a fruit of the Spirit.

What obedience costs is rarely dramatic. It costs comfort, certainty, and the approval of people who would have preferred the easier version of you. But it is the only path that leads anywhere real — and every person I have watched build a durable faith has paid that toll long before anyone called it courage.

Hearing God

Hearing God in a Loud Season

6 min read · Hearing God

You don't need more silence to hear God. You need a heart that has been trained to recognise His voice inside the noise — because for most of us, the noise isn't going anywhere.

People often tell me they've stopped hearing from God, when what's actually happened is they've stopped expecting to. Somewhere along the way, hearing God got reduced to a dramatic, audible experience — and when that didn't happen on schedule, they assumed the line had gone dead.

But scripture is full of people who heard God in the most unremarkable of circumstances — Elijah in a whisper after the wind, the fire, and the earthquake had all passed. The voice was there the whole time. It simply wasn't shouting.

If your season is loud — deadlines, family pressure, financial strain — don't wait for the noise to stop before you expect to hear from God. Train your ear inside the noise. Build small, repeatable habits of attention: a few unhurried minutes with scripture, a single honest prayer before you check your phone. God has never needed silence to speak. He has only ever needed our attention.

Purpose

Purpose Is Not a Feeling

7 min read · Purpose

Waiting to "feel called" before you obey is the slowest way to discover what you were actually made for. Purpose is far more often revealed in motion than in stillness.

I meet a lot of gifted, intelligent young people who are stuck — not because they lack ability, but because they're waiting for a feeling of certainty that purpose was never designed to give in advance. They want to feel called before they commit to anything, and so they commit to nothing, and the feeling never comes because there's no movement to confirm it against.

Almost every clear sense of purpose I've ever seen in someone's life arrived after a season of faithful, unglamorous obedience — not before it. Joseph didn't feel his way into ruling Egypt; he was faithful in a prison cell first. David didn't feel his way into the throne; he was faithful with sheep first.

If you're waiting to feel sure before you start, stop waiting. Start with the next obedient step in front of you, however small, and let purpose catch up to your obedience instead of asking it to go first.

Faith

The Faith That Doesn't Need an Audience

5 min read · Faith

Ask yourself honestly: how much of your faith would survive if no one ever found out about it? That question has a way of separating conviction from performance.

We live in a season where faith has become unusually visible — posted, captioned, shared. There is nothing wrong with that on its own. But it has quietly trained many believers to associate spiritual growth with spiritual visibility, and the two are not the same thing.

The faith that lasts is rarely the faith that performs well. It's the faith that keeps a quiet appointment with God on an ordinary Tuesday when there's no service to attend and no one to impress. Jesus himself instructed His followers to pray in private, not because public prayer is wrong, but because He knew the danger of building a faith that only exists when it's being observed.

Test your own faith with this question often: would it survive in a room with no audience? If the honest answer is uncertain, that's not condemnation — it's an invitation to rebuild something sturdier.

Leadership

Leading From a Place You'd Rather Not Go

9 min read · Leadership · Obedience

The leaders who shape people the most deeply are rarely the ones with the clearest five-year plan. They're the ones who have already been somewhere they didn't want to go.

I have never trusted leadership advice from someone who has not personally been broken by something. Not because suffering is required for competence, but because the leaders who have walked through real loss tend to lead with a different posture — less certain of themselves, more certain of God, and far more patient with the people they're leading.

If you are currently leading from a place you didn't choose — a diagnosis, a failure, a season of grief — resist the urge to hide it from the people watching you. Your willingness to lead honestly from that place may end up shaping them more than any strategy you could have planned for.

Leadership that has never knelt has nothing to stand on. Let the place you'd rather not be become the place that teaches you how to actually lead.
